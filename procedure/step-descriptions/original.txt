
Approval period begins

This step means that the approval period for the instrument has started.
The approval period is the time available for both Houses to approve an instrument subject to the made affirmative procedure. It usually begins when the instrument is made (signed into law). The number of days for the approval period and how to calculate it is defined in the parent Act. It is often 28 or 40 days.


Approval period ends

This step means that the approval period for the instrument has ended.
The approval period is the time available for both Houses to approve an instrument subject to the made affirmative procedure. If the instrument is not approved in the approval period then it means it can no longer be approved.

It begins when the instrument is made (signed into law). The number of days for the approval period and how to calculate it is defined in the parent Act. It is often 28 or 40 days.


Certified as England and Wales only under the English votes for English laws (EVEL) process

This step means that the Speaker of the House of Commons has certified the instrument as relating exclusively to England and Wales and is within devolved legislative competence (Standing Order No. 38P).

This means that if there is a division on a motion relating to the instrument, it will be subject to double-majority voting.


Certified as England only under the English votes for English laws (EVEL) process

This step means that the Speaker of the House of Commons has certified the instrument as relating exclusively to England and is within devolved legislative competence (Standing Order No. 38P).

This means that if there is a division on a motion relating to the instrument, it will be subject to double-majority voting.


Certified as England, Wales and Northern Ireland only under the English votes for English laws (EVEL) process

This step means that the Speaker of the House of Commons has certified the instrument as relating exclusively to England, Wales and Northern Ireland and is within devolved legislative competence (Standing Order No. 38P).

This means that if there is a division on a motion relating to the instrument, it will be subject to double-majority voting.


Chamber debate

This step means that a debate on a motion about the instrument happened in the Chamber of the House [of X]. At the end of the debate, a decision on the motion has to be made.


Chamber debate did not happen

This step means that a Chamber debate was scheduled for a specific date but it did not happen on that date. This can happen for various reasons.


Committee sifting period begins

This step means that the committee sifting period has started. The committee sifting period begins when a proposed negative statutory instrument is laid.

The EU (Withdrawal) Act 2018 set up a new sifting system for Parliament to consider whether the choice of procedure for a statutory instrument is appropriate, as Ministers can choose in some circumstances. A committee in each House – the European Statutory Instruments Committee in the Commons, and the Secondary Legislation Scrutiny Committee in the Lords – have 10 sitting days to report back whether they agree with the Minister’s choice of procedure or disagree.


Committee sifting period ends

This step means that the committee sifting period has ended.

The committee sifting period ends 10 sitting days after a proposed negative statutory instrument is laid, beginning with the day after the laid date. The 10 sitting days for the House of Commons and House of Lords are calculated separately and whichever is latest is the end of the committee sifting period.

Concerns raised by the JCSI

This step means that the Joint Committee on Statutory Instruments (JCSI) has drawn the instrument to the special attention of the House of Commons and the House of Lords. JCSI does this on a number of technical grounds (so not to with the merits of the policy), including defective drafting, unexpected use of powers, and requiring clarification.


Concerns raised by the SLSC

This step means that the Secondary Legislation Scrutiny Committee (SLSC) has drawn the instrument to the special attention of the House of Lords. SLSC examines the merits of the policy behind the instrument, and chooses to raises concerns on a number of grounds, such as if it is politically or legally important, may imperfectly achieve its policy objectives, or if the explanatory material is insufficient.


Concerns raised by the SCSI

This step means that the Select Committee on Statutory Instruments (JCSI) has drawn the instrument to the special attention of the House of Commons. SCSI does this on a number of technical grounds (so not to with the merits of the policy), including defective drafting, unexpected use of powers, and requiring clarification.


Considered by ESIC

This step means that the instrument was considered by the European Statutory Instruments Committee (ESIC). ESIC will decide at the end of consideration to either agree that the instrument should follow the negative procedure or recommend that the instrument should follow the affirmative procedure.


Considered by JCSI

This step means that the instrument was considered by the Joint Committee on Statutory Instruments (JCSI). JCSI will decide at the end of consideration to either raise concerns or not raise concerns.


Considered by SLSC

This step means that the instrument was considered by the Secondary Legislation Scrutiny Committee (SLSC). SLSC will decide at the end of consideration to either raise concerns or not raise concerns.


Considered by SCSI

This step means that the instrument was considered by the Select Committee on Statutory Instruments (SCSI). SCSI will decide at the end of consideration to either raise concerns or not raise concerns.


Considered for EVEL

This step means that the instrument has been considered for EVEL by the Speaker of the House of Commons. The Speaker will either decide to certify or not certify the instrument after considering it.

Delegated Legislation Committee (DLC) debate
 
This step means a debate happened in a Delegated Legislation Committee (DLC). A DLC is a general committee of 16-18 MPs who debate a motion to consider the instrument. No decision on whether to approve, reject, or stop the instrument can be made in a DLC. Those motions can only be moved in the Chamber.
